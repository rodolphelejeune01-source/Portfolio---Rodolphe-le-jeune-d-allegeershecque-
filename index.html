Strategic Culture

Origin and evolution

Snyder (1977) coined the term to explain Soviet preferences in nuclear strategy, defining it as the addition of “ideas, conditioned responses and habitual behaviours” of a strategic community. Three generations are often identified: (1) descriptive national ways of war; (2) Gramscian/discursive legitimisation; (3) constructivist/rigorous approaches focusing on organisational/service culture and measurable attributes.

Applications

Strategic culture refines predictions on risk tolerance and escalation, enabling tailored deterrence by matching messages, thresholds, and reputational cues. It also matters in insurgency and civil war contexts that reward proximity, direct communication networks, and narrative control (e.g., Russia & Iran pursuing narrow, regime‑centric aims with hybrid means).

Domestic & military culture

  • Domestic culture: U.S. idealism → maximalist goals; Russia/Iran → narrow, regime‑centric aims.
  • Military culture: U.S. forces optimise for interstate fights; Russia/Iran institutionalise hybrid/proxy methods.
  • Cultural gap: U.S. intervenes in distant, alien contexts; Russia/Iran operate nearby with dense social/religious/economic ties.

Pitfalls

  • Avoid tautology (defining cultures by the behaviours they produce).
  • Avoid reductionism/holism; pair cultural analysis with clear measures and material factors.

Bottom line

Consider culture rigorously alongside ground realities; align means and goals to design operations based on the adversary’s perspectives.

Week 6 — Comment on Crenshaw (1981), The Causes of Terrorism

Crenshaw treats terrorism as purposeful political behaviour within a three‑level framework: situational context, organisational strategy, and individual participation. Strengths: restores agency/trade‑offs at the organisational level (terrorism chosen under high asymmetry and closed alternatives) and catalogues mechanisms (provocation, intimidation, attrition, outbidding, publicity), warning about action‑reaction spirals. Updates: shift toward networked/leaderless structures; digital platforms amplify mobilisation; complement rational‑choice with sacred values/identity dynamics. Strategy checklist: close permissive conditions; distinguish manipulable vs structural grievances; anticipate organisational incentives; create off‑ramps; measure success by raising opportunity costs and widening nonviolent appeal.

Week 8 — Irregular warfare: Insurgency

Terrorism vs insurgency. Terrorism: sustained violence (often vs civilians/symbolic targets) to coerce/mobilise/attract attention. Insurgency: broader project to displace/expel authorities by organising through the population. Mechanisms: provocation, intimidation, attrition/disruption, outbidding, narrative mobilisation. Decisive variables: time, space, legitimacy.

Core thesis & "four laws". Population is paramount; active minority mobilises neutral majority; support is conditional; concentrate effort area‑by‑area (clear–hold–build–expand).

Invasion → resistance. If decapitation fails, urbanisation, territorial defence, and external supply favour protracted resistance. Russian reliance on BTGs/artillery/coercive occupation risks attrition/logistics strain/civilian backlash.

Implications for Ukraine & supporters. Expect hybrid conflict; prioritise distributed defence, governance continuity, information legitimacy; align material aid with political will; plan for the long term; avoid overreaction that fuels insurgency.

Summary synthesis: insurgency & strategy

Irregular war is a political struggle for legitimacy among civilians. Effective CT/COIN marries intelligence‑led policing and disciplined force to political action (reform, services, splits, information ops) with restraint and continuous learning. Galula provides the operating system (population‑centric, conditional support, area‑by‑area consolidation). Kilcullen illustrates resistance logic in Ukraine. Overlaying this, the “Second Nuclear Age” warns of escalation risks in multipolar contexts. Bottom line: align ends and means, secure civilians first, privilege legitimacy, and design long‑horizon campaigns that reduce the payoff of violence.
